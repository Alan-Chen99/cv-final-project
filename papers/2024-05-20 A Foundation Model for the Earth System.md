## Abstract

Reliable forecasts of the Earth system are crucial for human progress and safety from natural disasters. Artificial intelligence offers substantial potential to improve prediction accuracy and computational efficiency in this field, however this remains underexplored in many domains. Here we introduce Aurora, a large-scale foundation model for the Earth system trained on over a million hours of diverse data. Aurora outperforms operational forecasts for air quality, ocean waves, tropical cyclone tracks, and high-resolution weather forecasting at orders of magnitude smaller computational expense than dedicated existing systems. With the ability to fine-tune Aurora to diverse application domains at only modest computational cost, Aurora represents significant progress in making actionable Earth system predictions accessible to anyone.

## 1. Introduction

Earth system forecasts are indispensable tools for human societies, as evidenced by recent natural events such as the floods in Valencia, the air quality crisis in New Delhi, and hurricanes Helene and Milton in the eastern United States. Such systems not only provide crucial early warnings for extreme events, but are also invaluable for diverse fields ranging from agriculture to healthcare to global commerce.

Despite their vital role, Earth system forecasting models face several limitations. They are computationally demanding, often requiring purpose-built supercomputers and dedicated engineering teams. Their complexity complicates rapid improvements and necessitates substantial time and expertise for effective management. Finally, forecasting models incorporate numerous approximations, such as those for sub-grid scale processes, limiting accuracy.

Machine learning provides an attractive toolbox for addressing these issues. In 2023, a breakthrough came with Pangu-Weather [bi2023accurate], where a neural network replaced a numerical solver, outperforming state-of-the-art forecasting systems and sparking a wave of AI-based weather prediction models [chen2023fuxi; lam2023graphcast; han2024fengwu; chen2023fengwu]. These advancements have mostly centred on global medium-range weather forecasting at 0.25 deg resolution, leaving substantial gaps in other essential areas, including ocean dynamics, wave modelling, and atmospheric chemistry.

Aurora achieves state-of-the-art performance in the following critical forecasting domains:

- 5-day global air pollution forecasts at 0.4 deg resolution, outperforming resource-intensive numerical atmospheric chemistry simulations on 74% of targets
- 10-day global ocean wave forecasts at 0.25 deg resolution, exceeding costly numerical models on 86% of targets
- 5-day tropical cyclone track forecasts, outperforming seven operational forecasting centres on 100% of targets
- 10-day global weather forecasts at 0.1 deg resolution, surpassing state-of-the-art NWP models on 92% of targets while improving performance on extreme events

## 2. Aurora: A Flexible 3D Foundation Model for the Earth System

Aurora is a machine learning model that can ingest and make forecasts for any collection of Earth system variables at any desired resolution. The model consists of three parts: (1) an encoder which converts heterogeneous inputs into a universal latent three-dimensional (3D) representation; (2) a processor which evolves the representation in time; and (3) a decoder which translates the standard 3D representation back into the desired predictions. The processor is implemented as a 3D Swin Transformer [liu2021swin; dosovitskiy2020image] and the encoder and decoder as Perceiver-based modules [jaegle2021perceiver; jaegle2021perceiver_io]. Forecasts for different lead times are generated by recursively feeding predictions back into the model as inputs.

Aurora is pretrained on a mixture of forecasts, analysis data, reanalysis data, and climate simulations. Pretraining is expensive and requires vast amounts of data; fine-tuning is much cheaper and can typically be performed with little data. The pretraining objective is to minimise the next time-step (6-hour lead time) mean absolute error (MAE) for 150k steps on 32 A100 GPUs, corresponding to approximately two and a half weeks of training.

Aurora achieves unprecedented performance by simultaneously scaling data diversity and model size. Pretraining on more diverse data systematically improves validation performance as more datasets are added, especially for extreme values. Validation performance improves by approximately 6% for every 10x increase in model size. Aurora outperforms both IFS [ifs], the state-of-the-art NWP system, and GraphCast [lam2023graphcast] on over 91% of all targets.

## 3. Modelling Atmospheric Chemistry and Air Quality

Air quality, a crucial factor in human health, is determined by atmospheric concentrations of specific gases and aerosols [world2021global]. Forecasting atmospheric composition is significantly more complex and computationally costly than weather forecasting. It involves modelling complex chemical reactions through hundreds of stiff equations and accounting for anthropogenic emissions that drive heterogeneous pollution levels globally [brasseur2017modeling].

The Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS) extends the ECMWF's Integrated Forecasting System (IFS) with additional modules for aerosols, reactive gases, and greenhouse gases, increasing computational costs by approximately a factor of ten. To date, no AI method has attempted to produce operational predictions for global atmospheric composition at this scale.

Six air pollutants are the main drivers of poor air quality: carbon monoxide (CO), nitrogen oxide (NO), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulphur dioxide (SO2), ozone (O3), and particulate matter at 1 um (PM1), 2.5 um (PM2.5), and 10 um (PM10). Aurora models these across atmospheric levels and as total column (TC) values, with CAMS analysis taken to be ground truth. Fine-tuning uses CAMS analysis data from Oct 2017 to May 2022, with testing on May 2022 to Nov 2022.

**Results.** Aurora is competitive with CAMS (within 20% RMSE) on 95% of all targets, and matches or outperforms CAMS on 74% of all targets. At the three-day mark, Aurora matches or outperforms CAMS on 89% of all variables. Aurora generates predictions in approximately 1.1 s per hour lead time on a single A100 GPU, yielding roughly a 50,000x speed-up over CAMS. Fine-tuning the pretrained model produces gains over training from scratch with an average magnitude of 54%.

**Case study: Iraq sandstorm.** On 13 June 2022, Iraq was hit by a severe sandstorm, part of a series that led to over 5,000 hospitalizations. Aurora accurately predicts the sandstorm one day in advance with similar accuracy to CAMS, at a fraction of the computational cost.

## 4. Modeling Ocean Wave Dynamics

Accurate ocean wave forecasts are critical for shipping, coastal defenses, aquaculture, off-shore energy generation and disaster preparedness. The IFS High RESolution WAve Model (HRES-WAM) system [hres-wam-dataset] produces state-of-the-art wave forecasts up to ten days lead time. No AI model has yet attempted to produce operational predictions for global wave forecasts at this scale.

Key variables include significant wave height (SWH), mean wave period (MWP), and mean wave direction (MWD), each predicted for wind waves, total swell, primary swell, and secondary swell. Aurora is simultaneously fine-tuned on both wave and meteorological variables. Since HRES-WAM variables are undefined over land and wherever sea ice is present, Aurora is extended to support missing data via density channels [Gordon_2020_Convolutional_Conditional_Neural_Processes].

**Results.** Aurora is competitive with HRES-WAM (within 20% RMSE) on 96% of all targets, and matches or outperforms HRES-WAM on 86% of all wave variables. At the three-day mark, Aurora matches or outperforms HRES-WAM on 91% of all variables.

**Case study: Typhoon Nanmadol.** Aurora accurately predicts significant wave height and mean wave direction for Typhoon Nanmadol (the most intense tropical cyclone in 2022), with large waves in the typhoon accurately captured.

## 5. Predicting Tropical Cyclone Tracks

Tropical cyclones have caused more than 1.4 trillion USD in damage since 1950. Official forecasts of tropical cyclone tracks are produced by running multiple dynamical and statistical models, whose output is analyzed by human forecasters. Aurora, fine-tuned to HRES T0 at 0.25 deg, outperforms these complex systems for multiple agencies on all tropical cyclones globally in 2022-2023.

Track forecasts are generated using a simple heuristic tracker labelling the centre fix of the vortex as the minimum mean sea level pressure in consecutive predictions. Comparisons span four basins: National Hurricane Center (North Atlantic and Eastern Pacific), multiple agencies in the Northwest Pacific (PGTW, CWA, BABJ, RJTD, RKSL), and Australian Bureau of Meteorology.

**Results.** For all agencies and lead-times Aurora outperforms the official track forecast. In the North Atlantic and Eastern Pacific, improvements are 6% at one day and 20-25% at two to five days. On average, Aurora is 20% better in the North Atlantic and East Pacific, 18% in the West Pacific, and 24% in the Australian region. This is the first time that a machine learning model has surpassed full operational tropical cyclone forecasts up to five days.

**Case study: Typhoon Doksuri.** In 2023, Aurora accurately predicts Doksuri will make landfall in the Northern Philippines at four days lead time, while official predictions centre the vortex off the coast of Northern Taiwan. Doksuri became the costliest Pacific typhoon to date, inflicting more than 28 billion USD in damage.

## 6. High-Resolution Operational Weather Forecasting

HRES, the high-resolution configuration of the IFS, operates at approximately 0.1 deg (TCo1279 Gaussian grid). Current state-of-the-art AI weather prediction models operate only at 0.25 deg due to the wealth of data at that resolution, whereas 0.1 deg data only goes back to 2016. Aurora demonstrates that a pretraining-fine-tuning protocol can efficiently adapt to 0.1 deg and surpass IFS HRES.

Aurora is fine-tuned to 0.1 deg IFS HRES analysis data (2016-2022). Evaluation follows the operational protocol from [benbouallegue2023it], initialising Aurora with IFS HRES analysis and evaluating against IFS HRES analysis.

**Results.** Aurora achieves lower RMSE than IFS HRES on 92% of target variables, pressure levels and lead times. Performance gains are most pronounced at lead times of more than 12 hours (up to 24% RMSE reduction). Aurora outperforms IFS HRES against in-situ measurements of 10 m wind speed and 2 m temperature from the WeatherReal-ISD dataset (over 13,000 weather stations globally) for all lead times up to 10 days. Pretraining improves over training from scratch by 25%.

**Case study: Storm Ciaran.** A high-impact mid-latitude storm across North-West Europe in late 2023, resulting in the lowest recorded pressure in November in England. Among AI weather prediction models tested (FourCastNet, GraphCast, Pangu-Weather), Aurora is the only one capable of accurately predicting the abrupt rise in maximum 10 m wind speed, closely matching IFS analysis.

## 7. Discussion

Aurora outperforms multiple specialised operational prediction systems at a fraction of the computational cost, with state-of-the-art results for air quality, ocean waves, tropical cyclone tracks, and high-resolution weather forecasting. Each fine-tuning experiment took a small team 4-8 weeks, compared to multiple years for dynamical baseline models.

Improvements are possible along several axes:
- **Ensemble forecasts**: Aurora can be extended to generate ensembles, crucial for uncertain predictions [price2023gencast; lessig2023atmorep; li2024generative]
- **Scaling**: Performance ceiling has not been reached; more diverse data and larger models should improve results
- **End-to-end forecasting**: Aurora could be extended to directly operate on observational data, following advances in end-to-end weather forecasting [vaughan2024aardvarkweatherendtoenddatadriven]
- **Broader applications**: Ocean circulation, local weather, seasonal weather, vegetation, extreme events (floods, wildfires), agricultural productivity, renewable energy, sea ice extent

## 8. Methods

### 8.1 Problem Statement

The observed state of the atmosphere and surface at time $t$ is represented as $X^t \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times H \times W}$, where $V$ is the total number of variables and $H$, $W$ are latitude and longitude coordinates. The state splits into surface ($S^t \in \mathbb{R}^{V_S \times H \times W}$) and atmospheric ($A^t \in \mathbb{R}^{V_A C \times H \times W}$) components, with $V_S$ surface variables, $V_A$ atmospheric variables, and $C$ pressure levels.

A simulator $\Phi: (\mathbb{R}^{V \times H \times W})^2 \to \mathbb{R}^{V \times H \times W}$ maps states at times $t-1$ and $t$ to predicted state $\hat{X}^{t+1}$. For longer predictions, the simulator is applied autoregressively:

$$\hat{X}^{t+k} = \Phi(\hat{X}^{t+k-2}, \hat{X}^{t+k-1})$$

### 8.2 The Aurora Model

**3D Perceiver encoder.** The encoder treats all variables as $H \times W$ images. Static variables (orography, land-sea mask, soil-type mask) are appended as extra surface-level variables. Images are split into $P \times P$ patches, mapped to $D$-dimensional embeddings via variable-specific linear transformations. Embeddings are summed per level and tagged with additive pressure level encodings. A Perceiver module reduces $C$ physical pressure levels to $L=3$ latent pressure levels. The resulting $\frac{H}{P} \times \frac{W}{P} \times L$ representation is tagged with position, patch area, and absolute time encodings using a Fourier expansion scheme.

**Multi-scale 3D Swin Transformer U-Net backbone.** A 3D Swin Transformer U-Net serves as the neural simulator. Key features:
- Symmetric upsampling-downsampling structure with three stages each, enabling multi-scale processing
- 3D Swin Transformer layers performing local self-attention within windows (size 2x12x6), emulating local computations in numerical integration
- Window shifting every other layer for information propagation between neighboring regions, with spherical topology handling
- Res-post-norm layer normalization for training stability
- No positional bias (enabling multi-resolution operation)
- 48 layers across three stages (backbone encoder: 6, 10, 8 layers; decoder: 8, 10, 6 layers), compared to 16 layers in Pangu-Weather

**3D Perceiver decoder.** Reverses the encoder operations: deaggregates latent pressure levels to any desired collection using a Perceiver layer, then dynamically decodes into patches via variable-specific linear layers. This allows output at arbitrary pressure levels for an arbitrary set of variables.

**Position, scale, level, and time encodings.** All encodings use a Fourier scheme:

$$\text{Emb}(x) = [\cos\frac{2\pi x}{\lambda_i}, \sin\frac{2\pi x}{\lambda_i}] \text{ for } 0 \leq i < D/2$$

with log-spaced wavelengths between $\lambda_{\min}$ and $\lambda_{\max}$:
- Position: average latitude/longitude of each patch ($\lambda_{\min} = 0.01$, $\lambda_{\max} = 720$)
- Scale: patch area $A = R^2(\sin\phi_2 - \sin\phi_1)(\theta_2 - \theta_1)$, enabling multi-resolution operation
- Pressure level: atmospheric pressure value ($\lambda_{\min} = 0.01$, $\lambda_{\max} = 10000$)
- Absolute time: hours since Jan 1, 1970 ($\lambda_{\min} = 1$ hour, $\lambda_{\max} = 1$ year)

**Model configurations.** The 1.3B parameter model uses embedding dimension 512 (doubling each stage), 64-dimensional attention heads, and 16 Perceiver cross-attention heads.

| Model size | Backbone encoder layers | Backbone decoder layers | Embed dim. | Attn head dim. | Perceiver heads |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 117M | (2, 6, 2) | (2, 6, 2) | 256 | 64 | 8 |
| 290M | (4, 8, 4) | (4, 8, 4) | 320 | 64 | 8 |
| 660M | (6, 8, 8) | (8, 8, 6) | 384 | 64 | 12 |
| 1.3B | (6, 10, 8) | (8, 10, 6) | 512 | 64 | 16 |

### 8.3 Training Methods

**Training objective.** Mean absolute error (MAE) with variable-specific and dataset-specific weights:

$$\mathcal{L}(\hat{X}^t, X^t) = \frac{\gamma}{V_S + V_A}\Big[\alpha\sum_{k=1}^{V_S}\frac{w^S_k}{HW}\sum_{i,j}|\hat{S}^t_{k,i,j} - S^t_{k,i,j}| + \beta\sum_{k=1}^{V_A}\frac{1}{CHW}\sum_c w^A_{k,c}\sum_{i,j}|\hat{A}^t_{k,c,i,j} - A^t_{k,c,i,j}|\Big]$$

where $\alpha = 1/4$ (surface weight), $\beta = 1$ (atmospheric weight), $\gamma$ is dataset-specific ($\gamma_{\text{ERA5}} = 2.0$, $\gamma_{\text{GFS-T0}} = 1.5$, rest 1.0).

**Pretraining.** 150k steps on 32 A100 GPUs (batch size 1 per GPU), approximately 2.5 weeks. Half-cosine decay with 1k linear warm-up, base learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$, AdamW optimizer with weight decay $5 \times 10^{-6}$, drop path probability 0.2, bf16 mixed precision. Activation checkpointing and gradient sharding for memory efficiency.

**Short-lead-time fine-tuning.** Full architecture fine-tuned through 1-2 roll-out steps, task-dependent hyperparameters. Examples:
- HRES 0.25 deg: 8k steps on 8 GPUs, two roll-out steps, LR $5 \times 10^{-5}$
- HRES 0.1 deg: 12.5k steps on 8 GPUs, single step (memory constraints), LR $2 \times 10^{-4}$
- CAMS 0.4 deg: Two-stage (CAMS reanalysis then CAMS analysis), 49.5k total steps
- HRES-WAM 0.25 deg: 24k total steps with data range adjustment for IFS cycle change

**Roll-out fine-tuning.** Uses Low Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [hu2021lora] on backbone self-attention linear layers ($Wx + BAx$). Key innovations:
- "Pushforward trick" [brandstetter2022message]: gradients only through last roll-out step
- In-memory replay buffer (inspired by deep RL [lin_experience_replay; Mnih2015HumanlevelCT]): samples initial conditions, computes predictions, adds back to buffer, periodically refreshes from dataset
- This avoids expensive full roll-outs at each training step

### 8.4 Task-Specific Adaptations

**Extensions for 0.1 deg.** Patch size increased from 4 to 10 (matching the 2.5x resolution increase so backbone input size is preserved). Bilinear interpolation of weights with magnitude scaling for transfer. Two backbone layers removed from middle stages to fit in memory.

**Extensions for air pollution.** 12-hour model for diurnal cycles. Differencing: for variables with clear diurnal cycles, predict difference w.r.t. 24h ago; otherwise w.r.t. 12h ago. Modulation factor in decoder:

$$\hat{X}^t = [\Phi_{\text{mod}}(X^{t-1}, X^{t-2}) + 1] \cdot X^a + \Phi_{\text{pred}}(X^{t-1}, X^{t-2})$$

Log-transform for concentration variables with large dynamic range:

$$x_{\text{transformed}} = c_1 \min(x, 2.5) + c_2 \frac{\log(\max(x, 10^{-4})) - \log(10^{-4})}{\log(10^{-4})}$$

Additional static variables: time of day, day of week, day of year (as sine/cosine), monthly average anthropogenic emissions for ammonia, CO, NOx, SO2.

**Extensions for wave forecasting.** Angle-valued variables transformed via $x \mapsto (\sin(x), \cos(x))$. Density channels for missing data: binary indicator (present/absent) for each wave variable. Bathymetry and HRES-WAM domain mask as additional static variables.

### 8.5 Datasets

Training and evaluation use five main categories:
- **Analysis data**: Best estimate at time of forecast (HRES analysis, HRES T0, GFS T0, CAMS analysis)
- **Reanalysis data**: Best historical estimate using future observations (ERA5, MERRA-2, CAMS reanalysis EAC4)
- **Forecast data**: Operational predictions (HRES, GFS, CAMS)
- **Reforecast data**: Retrospective forecasts from reanalysis initial conditions (GEFS)
- **Climate simulations**: Physics/chemistry/biology modeling of potential scenarios (CMIP6: CMCC-CM2-VHR4 at 0.25 deg, ECMWF-IFS-HR at 0.45 deg)

Standard pressure levels (13 from WeatherBench2): 50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, 850, 925, 1000 hPa.

| Variable | Type | Units | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2T | Surface | K | Temperature at 2m |
| U10, V10 | Surface | m/s | Wind components at 10m |
| MSL | Surface | Pa | Mean sea level pressure |
| U, V | Atmospheric | m/s | Wind components |
| T | Atmospheric | K | Temperature |
| Q | Atmospheric | kg/kg | Specific humidity |
| Z | Atmospheric | m2/s2 | Geopotential |

### 8.6 Verification Metrics

**RMSE** (latitude-weighted):
$$\text{RMSE} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \sqrt{\frac{1}{HW}\sum_{i,j} w(i)(\hat{X}^t_{i,j} - X^t_{i,j})^2}$$

where $w(i) = \cos(\text{lat}(i)) / [\frac{1}{H}\sum_{i'}\cos(\text{lat}(i'))]$.

**ACC** (anomaly correlation coefficient): Correlation between prediction and ground truth deviations from daily climatology.

**Thresholded RMSE** for extreme events: RMSE computed only on gridpoints exceeding threshold $b_{i,j} = \mu_{i,j} + g \cdot \sigma_{i,j}$, where $\mu$, $\sigma$ are ERA5 mean and standard deviation.

## 9. Data Diversity and Model Scaling

### Data Scaling

Four pretraining configurations (C1-C4):
- C1: ERA5 only
- C2: C1 + CMIP6 (CMCC, IFS-HR)
- C3: C2 + IFS ensemble, HRES forecasts, IFS ensemble mean
- C4: Alternative to C3 with better atmospheric coverage and higher quality forecast data

Adding climate simulation data (C2) improves over ERA5-only (C1) across almost all variables. Configuration C4 performs best overall and is used for all main results. Data diversity provides up to 35% error reduction in the tails of the distribution.

### Model Scaling

Four model sizes (117M, 290M, 660M, 1.3B) pretrained on C4 dataset mixture. At 5k GPU hours, validation loss follows $\mathcal{L}(N) \propto N^{-0.026}$, corresponding to 6% reduction per 10x increase in model size. Bigger models achieve lower validation loss for the same compute budget.

## 10. Comparison at 0.25 deg Resolution

### Aurora vs GraphCast

Aurora consistently outperforms GraphCast across the vast majority of targets. Performance gains are most pronounced at lead times past 3 days and for upper atmospheric levels (up to 40% RMSE reduction). Large improvements (10-15%) are observed for temperature, geopotential height, and wind velocity. Aurora's advantages are attributed to: (1) data diversity, (2) model size (1.3B vs 37M parameters), (3) foundation model approach, (4) two-stage fine-tuning with LoRA.

### Aurora vs Pangu-Weather

Aurora consistently outperforms Pangu across all variables and pressure levels.

### Aurora vs IFS HRES at 0.25 deg

Aurora largely outperforms HRES but still performs worse at the top of the atmosphere. Aurora outperforms the IFS ensemble mean up to 7 days lead time.

### Extreme Event Prediction

Aurora outperforms GraphCast on wind speed prediction across the distribution. At longer lead times, AIWP model performance relative to IFS HRES becomes markedly improved. Improvements extend to atmospheric variables at multiple pressure levels.
